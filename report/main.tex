\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Mathematical word problems are a clean test bed for reinforcement learning on language models because the answer is a single number, so a deterministic check replaces the learned reward model used in standard RLHF \citep{ouyang2022instructgpt}. The substantive shift in the last year is that reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) has gone from a curiosity to the dominant post-training recipe for reasoning models. DeepSeek-R1 \citep{deepseekr1} showed that a base language model can develop self-reflection and dynamic strategy adaptation through pure RL against correctness signals alone, with no supervised reasoning demonstrations. T\"ulu 3 \citep{lambert2024tulu3} reproduced the recipe at 8B and 70B on top of Llama 3.1, and the GRPO optimizer used by both papers comes from DeepSeekMath \citep{shao2024deepseekmath}.

What is not settled in the public literature is whether this recipe transfers cleanly to (i) small bases ($\sim$1.5B), (ii) parameter-efficient adaptation rather than full fine-tuning, and (iii) bases that have already been math-specialized through supervised pre-training. This report addresses those three questions on GSM8K \citep{cobbe2021} and reports a negative result for the second and third. We then describe a hierarchical Router--Solver agent in two variants, which falls well short of the linear-policy baselines and whose failure-mode taxonomy locates the bottleneck in the agent's interface contract rather than in the base model's reasoning capability.

Four concrete results come out of this report. On the general-purpose Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct base, LoRA-only GRPO adds about 1.9 points of GSM8K accuracy (Section~\ref{sec:linear}). On the math-specialized Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B-Instruct, the same recipe adds zero net points despite producing different reasoning text on every test problem (Section~\ref{sec:math-base}). Switching to DAPO-style dynamic group filtering with a larger LoRA on the same math base recovers the training signal: the policy reaches $88.5\%$ majority-vote accuracy ($94.1\%$ pass@8) with $K{=}8$ self-consistency (Section~\ref{sec:dapo}). A hierarchical Router--Solver agent at the same scale reaches at most about 35\% rollout accuracy, and roughly half of its failures are not reasoning errors but mismatches in the agent's plan, step, and final-answer contract (Section~\ref{sec:router-solver}).

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

Chain-of-thought prompting \citep{wei2022cot} showed that large language models can solve arithmetic and symbolic reasoning tasks by emitting intermediate steps before committing to an answer. Self-consistency \citep{wang2022sc} sampled multiple reasoning paths and majority-voted, adding 17.9 points on GSM8K. Our policy is a chain-of-thought policy by construction: the prompt asks the model to solve the problem step by step and place the final answer inside a \verb|\boxed{}| token. The benchmark is GSM8K \citep{cobbe2021}, 7{,}473 train and 1{,}319 test grade-school word problems with a single numeric answer each; \citet{cobbe2021} also introduced verifier-based test-time selection, which is the test-time analogue of RLVR.

The optimizer is Group Relative Policy Optimization \citep{shao2024deepseekmath}, a variant of PPO \citep{schulman2017ppo} that removes the critic by sampling $G$ rollouts per prompt and using the empirical mean reward in the group as the baseline. The advantage of rollout $i$ is $(r_i - \mathrm{mean}(r))/\mathrm{std}(r)$. This cuts memory roughly in half compared with PPO and is the optimizer DeepSeekMath \citep{shao2024deepseekmath} and DeepSeek-R1 \citep{deepseekr1} use at scale. We use a hard verifiable reward in the style of T\"ulu 3's RLVR \citep{lambert2024tulu3}: $+1$ for a correct boxed answer, $+0.5$ format bonus for a parseable boxed token, $0$ otherwise. Process-supervised rewards \citep{lightman2023verify} can outperform outcome supervision when step-level labels are available; GSM8K is outcome-only.

The policy is a LoRA adapter \citep{hu2021lora} on top of a frozen 1.5B base. LoRA trains low-rank update matrices and reduces trainable parameters by roughly four orders of magnitude, which is what lets the project run on a single GPU. The closest prior work is \citet{shao2024deepseekmath}: the same GRPO objective at 7B with full fine-tuning and roughly an order of magnitude more rollouts. Their 51.7\% on the competition-level MATH benchmark shows the recipe scales. Our contribution is the negative result at 1.5B with LoRA on a math-specialized base, which maps where the recipe stops being effective. Qwen2.5-Math \citep{yang2024qwen25math} provides the math-specialized 1.5B base we use in Sections~\ref{sec:math-base} and \ref{sec:dapo}, and ReAct \citep{yao2022react} is the structural ancestor of the hierarchical and graph-memory variants in the larger project plan.

DAPO \citep{yu2025dapo} is a variant of GRPO that filters out uninformative groups before committing to a gradient step: any group whose $G$ rollouts are all-correct or all-wrong is discarded and the batch is refilled by resampling new prompts until the target number of informative groups is collected (or a resample budget is exhausted). This guarantees that every policy update sees within-group reward variance, which is the necessary condition for a non-zero GRPO advantage. Section~\ref{sec:dapo} applies DAPO to address the dead-gradient failure mode diagnosed in Section~\ref{sec:math-base}.

\paragraph{Hardware and compute.} All training runs were executed on a single NVIDIA L4 GPU (24\,GB VRAM). The software stack is PyTorch with HuggingFace \texttt{transformers} and \texttt{peft} for LoRA adaptation; the DAPO run additionally used vLLM for rollout generation, syncing the updated LoRA adapter to vLLM after each gradient step. Table~\ref{tab:compute} summarizes rollout counts and approximate wall-clock times.

\section{Linear Reasoning Model}
\label{sec:linear}

\paragraph{Policy.} The policy is Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct with a LoRA adapter ($r{=}8$, $\alpha{=}16$, dropout 0.05) on the \verb|q_proj| and \verb|v_proj| projections of every attention block. The base weights are frozen, so the trainable parameter count is on the order of $10^{6}$ rather than the full 1.5\,B. Every prompt is wrapped in the model's chat template with a fixed system message (``You are a math tutor. Solve the problem step by step, showing your reasoning clearly. End with the final answer in \textbackslash boxed\{\} format.''), and rollouts decode at most 512 new tokens.

\paragraph{Reward and optimization.} Each completion receives a verifiable reward: $+1.0$ if the value in the final \verb|\boxed{}| matches the GSM8K ground truth, $+0.5$ format bonus for a parseable \verb|\boxed{}|, and $0$ otherwise. For each training question we draw $G{=}6$ rollouts at temperature 0.8 and form a GRPO group-relative advantage by normalizing rewards by the group mean and standard deviation. The policy is updated against a frozen copy of itself (the reference) with a KL penalty of $0.04$, using AdamW at learning rate $10^{-5}$ and a microbatch of 3 trajectories per forward/backward pass. We run one pass over $2{,}000$ GSM8K training questions ($12{,}000$ rollouts), saving a checkpoint every 500 questions. The run takes about seven hours on a single GPU.

\paragraph{Training dynamics.} Figure~\ref{fig:linear-train} shows training-time rollout accuracy. Rolling accuracy climbs from about 65\% to 80\% over the run, and the cumulative curve saturates near 78\%. Per-rollout reward moves from about 1.1 to 1.3 (out of a 1.5 ceiling), policy loss stays cleanly negative, and KL to the reference grows monotonically but stays bounded by the $0.04$ penalty. The cumulative training accuracy near 78\% lines up with the held-out 71.6\% in Table~\ref{tab:baselines} once the temperature gap between training (0.8) and evaluation (greedy) is accounted for.

\section{Linear RL on a Math-Specialized Base}
\label{sec:math-base}

A natural follow-up is whether the recipe of Section~\ref{sec:linear} compounds when stacked on Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B-Instruct \citep{yang2024qwen25math}, a base that has already been math-specialized through supervised pre-training. It does not. Running the identical architecture and GRPO recipe on top of the math base returned the post-training policy to exactly the base-model accuracy of $1118/1319 = 84.8\%$.

\paragraph{Setup.} Policy, prompt, reward, and optimizer are identical to Section~\ref{sec:linear}. The only changes are that the base model is Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B-Instruct, the LoRA configuration is unchanged so trainable parameters stay at $\sim$1.09M (0.07\% of the model), and the training set is a probe-derived $1{,}000$-question subset of GSM8K drawn from the union of \emph{trivial}, \emph{mixed}, and \emph{hard} buckets. Bucket membership is determined by how many of $G{=}4$ probe rollouts at $T{=}0.8$ from the same base model were correct. Training runs one epoch at $G{=}8$ rollouts per question, totalling $8{,}000$ rollouts.

\paragraph{Training dynamics.} Figure~\ref{fig:math-train} shows on-policy training accuracy and the KL signal. The contrast with Section~\ref{sec:linear} is sharp. Rolling accuracy oscillates inside $0.45$ to $0.65$ for the entire run with no upward trend, and the cumulative curve flatlines at $\sim$0.55 after about 150 steps. Per-decile rollout accuracy across the $1{,}000$-step run is $\{54.8, 56.7, 49.7, 55.8, 58.7, 56.7, 54.3, 54.9, 54.7, 54.8\}\%$, which is noise around a constant, not learning. The KL signal is the cleanest diagnostic: it jumps to $\sim$0.04 inside the first few steps and stays pinned there, meaning the $0.04$ penalty saturates immediately and the policy is held adjacent to the reference for the entire run.

\paragraph{Test-set result.} On the full 1319-question GSM8K test set, the post-training policy and the untouched base agree on the correctness label for $1{,}291/1{,}319 = 97.9\%$ of problems. They produce different reasoning text on every single problem ($0/1{,}319$ character-identical generations), so weights did move, but they moved in directions that flip $14$ test problems from wrong to right and a different $14$ from right to wrong. The net change is zero, and the $\pm 14$ swap is consistent with sampling noise at $T>0$.

\paragraph{Why the policy did not learn.} GRPO produces gradient only when, within a group of $G$ rollouts on the same question, some succeed and some fail. Groups that are all-correct or all-wrong have zero advantage and contribute nothing but the KL drag toward the reference. Three properties of this setup combine to leave very few informative groups. First, the base is already strong, solving roughly 85\% of GSM8K at $T{=}0$, so a large share of trivial and mixed-bucket questions yields all-correct $G{=}8$ groups even at $T{=}0.8$. Second, the residual hard tail is mostly all-wrong: for genuinely hard probe-bucket questions the base typically misses on every rollout, again giving zero variance. Third, LoRA capacity on \verb|q_proj| and \verb|v_proj| alone does not let the policy reach a meaningfully better policy than the base within the $0.04$ KL ball, and the KL trace pinning at the penalty value is exactly this constraint becoming active. On Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct (general) the same recipe did train because the base's $\sim$70\% accuracy left enough mixed-outcome groups for GRPO to find useful gradient. On the math-specialized base the informative-group rate is too low, so the policy is held at base for the entire run. The result rules out the simplest path to closing the 13-point gap from the trained general-base policy to the math-specialized base: applying the same LoRA recipe a second time. Larger LoRA rank, curriculum filtering to the mixed-outcome bucket, and a smaller KL penalty would each address a different identified bottleneck; Section~\ref{sec:dapo} tests all three together via DAPO-style training.

\section{DAPO on the Math-Specialized Base}
\label{sec:dapo}

Section~\ref{sec:math-base} identified three bottlenecks: an informative-group rate too low to sustain gradient, a KL budget small enough to pin the policy to the reference from step one, and a LoRA configuration covering too few projections. This section applies DAPO-style dynamic group filtering \citep{yu2025dapo} together with a wider LoRA and a looser KL penalty to address all three simultaneously.

\paragraph{Setup.} The base model and prompt format are identical to Section~\ref{sec:math-base}. Three hyperparameters change. (i)~LoRA rank increases from 8 to 32 and covers seven projection matrices (\verb|q,k,v,o,gate,up,down|), raising trainable parameters from $\sim$1.1M to $\sim$25M. (ii)~The KL coefficient drops from 0.04 to 0.015. (iii)~The training loop uses DAPO dynamic group filtering targeting 2 informative groups per gradient step with up to 8 resample attempts before skipping. Training uses the \verb|mixed_hard| bucket ($\sim$1{,}738 effective training examples), $G{=}4$ rollouts per question, and a per-token loss normalization. Over the full run, 688 informative groups were kept from $\sim$1{,}738 attempted questions (496 discarded as all-correct, 554 as all-wrong). Evaluation uses self-consistency with $K{=}8$ rollouts at $T{=}0.6$ and majority vote on the full 1{,}319-question GSM8K test set.

\paragraph{Training dynamics.} Figure~\ref{fig:dapo-train} shows rolling accuracy and KL over 330 gradient steps. The contrast with Figure~\ref{fig:math-train} is sharp on two dimensions. First, KL does not pin: it climbs monotonically from near zero to $\sim$0.0008 across the full run, well below the 0.015 penalty ceiling, meaning the optimizer had room to move throughout. Second, per-step reward rises from $\sim$0.65 to $\sim$1.01 (Figure~\ref{fig:dapo-train} shows accuracy; reward follows the same upward trend), confirming that the policy genuinely learns higher-reward completions on the filtered informative groups. Rolling accuracy on the \verb|mixed_hard| training subset oscillates in the 50--60\% range, which is the expected operating point: these are by construction the questions the base model cannot solve on every rollout, so 50--60\% on them is a meaningful signal rather than noise.

\paragraph{Why DAPO succeeded where vanilla GRPO did not.} In the vanilla GRPO run, roughly 70\% of groups were all-correct at step 0 on the math base, contributing only KL drag and no gradient. DAPO's filter raises the effective informative-group rate to 100\% by construction. The wider LoRA ($\sim$25M parameters across seven projection matrices) provides more representational freedom, and the lower KL coefficient (0.015) gives the optimizer room to use it. The KL trace in Figure~\ref{fig:dapo-train} is the cleanest diagnostic: it grows continuously rather than pinning, the opposite of the trace in Figure~\ref{fig:math-train}. Together the three changes flip a zero-learning run into one that achieves state-of-the-art self-consistency accuracy at this scale and parameter count.

\section{Hierarchical Router--Solver Agent}
\label{sec:router-solver}

The negative result in Section~\ref{sec:math-base} motivated a different policy class. Instead of a single chain of thought, the agent decomposes the problem into an explicit \emph{plan} from a Router and a sequence of \emph{step executions} from a Solver, both LoRA adapters on a shared base. We built two versions. Neither closed the gap to the linear baselines, and the failure modes locate the bottleneck in the interface contract rather than in the base model's reasoning.

\subsection{V1: code-generating Solver with heuristic rewards}
\label{sec:rsv1}

V1 used one Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct base with two LoRA adapters. The Router emitted a numbered subgoal list, the Solver wrote a Python snippet per subgoal, and a sandboxed Python tool executed it. The outcome reward was binary on the final numeric answer; the only intermediate signal was a heuristic format and tool-call-success bonus. Training used GRPO over $B \cdot G$ trajectories per step, sharing one optimizer between the two adapters. V1 reached approximately $1.7\%$ test accuracy. Most of the engineering effort went into infrastructure, including vLLM memory pressure, sequential versus batched rollout, gradient checkpointing, and an out-of-memory fallback chunked backward path. The result was a $\sim$4$\times$ throughput improvement, $235$ to $58$ seconds per step at $B{=}2, G{=}4$, but no movement on test accuracy. The dominant failure mode was outcome-only credit assignment over a multi-step tool-using pipeline: a wrong final answer back-propagates negative gradient through every Router subgoal and every Solver snippet that produced it, whether or not each individual step was correct. The Python-tool contract added a second brittle interface, since Solver code that printed the right value but failed an answer-extraction regex still scored zero. At 1.5B with rank-8 LoRA, the joint policy did not have either the capacity or the signal-to-noise ratio to learn this contract from outcome reward alone.

\subsection{V2: text-reasoning Solver with judge-based dense rewards}
\label{sec:rsv2}

\paragraph{Setup.} V2 replaced the Python Solver with a text-reasoning Solver and added a remote judge (GPT-4o-mini) that scored three quantities per rollout: a plan reward from the Router's JSON plan, a step-level reward for each Solver step, and a binary outcome reward on the final answer. GRPO then operated on a weighted advantage combining router, step, and outcome contributions, with the router weight decaying 5\% per epoch and the outcome weight increasing correspondingly. Judging was batched ten items per API call, bringing inference cost from $\sim$\$75 to $\sim$\$10 per full pass. Training used $G{=}4$ rollouts over $7{,}500$ questions for one epoch, roughly $30{,}000$ rollouts.

\paragraph{Result.} On a 20-question traced sample, raw rollout accuracy was 10.8\%. The best inference-side intervention, an answer-synthesis step that re-prompts the Solver with the full trajectory and asks for a single final number, lifted that to 20.0\%. A larger fresh-eval pass on the trained checkpoint reached about 35\% rollout accuracy with synthesis on. This is well above V1's 1.7\% but still about 50 points below the linear-policy baselines in Table~\ref{tab:baselines}.

\paragraph{Why V2 did not work.} Rather than guess, we built a per-rollout failure taxonomy on the traced $20\mathrm{Q} \times G{=}6$ sample and counted bucket frequencies. About 51.7\% of failed rollouts are not core reasoning failures but architectural failures of the Router--Solver contract: \texttt{copied\_intermediate\_as\_final} (15.8\%, the emitted final answer matches an earlier intermediate quantity), \texttt{correct\_number\_in\_trace\_wrong\_final} (12.5\%, the correct number appears in the rollout text but extraction picks a different number), \texttt{plan\_endpoint\_mismatch} (10.0\%, the last subgoal is not the global answer target), and \texttt{plan\_parse\_failed} (13.3\%, Router JSON does not parse). Only 37.5\% of failures were \texttt{wrong\_numeric\_final}, the Solver actually computing the wrong number. V2's ceiling is set by structural choices (last step equals final answer, JSON-parseable plans, outcome credit attached only to the last step's log-probs), not by an inability of the base to do grade-school arithmetic.

\paragraph{Attempted fixes that did not generalize.} Two interventions were tested directly. Router prompt hardening, a stricter template intended to drive \texttt{plan\_parse\_failed} to zero, caused a catastrophic regression on an isolated 10Q split (20.0\% to 3.3\% with only $9/60$ valid rollouts), because the harder prompt made the Router refuse to emit plans on out-of-template questions. Outcome credit on all steps, a training-side change that distributes the outcome reward across every Solver step's log-probs, improved in-loop training accuracy (15.0\% to 26.7\%) but lost on a fresh traced eval (35.0\% to 28.3\%): the variant overfit to sampled training trajectories and produced a weaker policy on fresh draws. The inference-side fix that did help, final-answer synthesis, works precisely because it bypasses the last-step-equals-final-answer assumption that the taxonomy identified as the largest single failure source.

\paragraph{Reading.} V2 is bottlenecked by the Router--Solver interface design, not by reasoning capability at this scale. The question-level taxonomy shows 25\% of questions already have at least one correct rollout in their $G{=}6$ group, with a projected 90\% if all non-core failures were eliminated. The base model produces usable reasoning often enough that the actual bottleneck is whether the system can extract a single correct scalar from a multi-step trajectory. At 1.5B with rank-8 LoRA, an outcome-only or step-judged reward cannot supervise that contract into shape. The next architectural change has to flatten or supervise the Router--Solver hand-offs rather than just decompose harder, which is the motivation for the graph-memory and hierarchical-RL extensions in the larger project plan.

\section{Benchmarks}
\label{sec:benchmarks}

\paragraph{Setup.} All three models are evaluated on the full GSM8K test set (1319 problems) under matched prompting and decoding. Every model receives the same chat-template system message used during training in Section~\ref{sec:linear}, so the comparison is not handicapped by prompt format. Answers are extracted by taking the last \verb|\boxed{N}| match in the completion, falling back to the last numeric token if no box is emitted.

\paragraph{Metrics.} We report three protocols. \emph{Greedy} decodes one completion per question with \texttt{do\_sample=False} and counts a question correct iff the extracted number matches ground truth. \emph{Majority-vote} (self-consistency, \citealt{wang2022sc}) draws $K$ rollouts at temperature $T$ (we use $K{=}8$, $T{=}0.6$) and counts the modal extracted answer. \emph{Pass@K} counts a question correct iff at least one of the $K$ rollouts is correct, and is the upper bound a perfect aggregator could reach. Table~\ref{tab:baselines} uses greedy for parity with canonical GSM8K numbers; the DAPO follow-up in Section~\ref{sec:dapo} reports majority-vote and pass@8 in Table~\ref{tab:dapo}.

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
    Model & Accuracy & Correct / Total \\
    \midrule
    Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct (general)       & 69.7\% & 920 / 1319 \\
    Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct + RLVR (ours)   & 71.6\% & 945 / 1319 \\
    Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B-Instruct (math)     & 84.8\% & 1118 / 1319 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \caption{GSM8K test accuracy under matched prompting and decoding. LoRA RL on the general base adds $+1.9$ percentage points; the math-pretrained variant remains far ahead.}
  \label{tab:baselines}
\end{table}

The $+1.9$ point gain over the general base matches the eval accuracy logged at the end of training (71.95\%) within rounding. The math-specialized base sits roughly 13 points above the trained policy, a gap that at this parameter count is more about pretraining corpus than about the RL objective. Sections~\ref{sec:math-base} and \ref{sec:router-solver} describe two attempts to close that gap: stacking the same RL recipe on top of the math base (zero net movement) and switching to a hierarchical Router--Solver policy class (a $\sim$50-point regression). Together they suggest that further progress at this scale needs structural changes to the policy class that supervise the answer-extraction contract directly, rather than additional RL on the same linear policy.
